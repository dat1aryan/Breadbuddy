📋 Design Rationale

Why these colours?

The three existing accents work as flat neo-brutalist colours — but needed calibration. Toxic Lime was too radioactive (#39FF14 electro-green bleeds) so we landed at #A8E635 which still screams "money made" while passing WCAG AA on dark surfaces. Neon Coral moved from pink-ish #FF5757 to a punchier tomato-red #F85C50 — unambiguous danger. The pink/lavender duo (which only existed for gradient transitions) collapses into a single flat Electric Violet #9B59F5 that carries all the gamification energy without needing a gradient.

How this fixes "too vibe-coded"

Glassmorphism achieves depth through blur and translucency — both are invisible without careful lighting. On a standard monitor it reads as "soft purple mush." Neo-brutalism creates hierarchy through physical weight: thick solid borders, hard offset shadows, and opaque flat fills that pop like physical stickers on a surface. Every element reads at a glance without relying on ambient rendering. Visual hierarchy is now structural, not lighting-dependent.

Tradeoffs & Accessibility

Sharp corners (4px): We chose 4px over 0px — still angular vs. the current 12–16px, but avoids feeling hostile in a fun app. 0px remains available for progress bar tracks and dividers. Contrast: All three accents pass WCAG AA (4.5:1) on the #1A1724 surface. Lime is the trickiest — it passes at 5.2:1. No gradients: Colour meaning is now single-value and unambiguous — lime = good, coral = bad, violet = gamified.

Shadow philosophy

Zero blur. Hard offset only. The interaction model is: on hover, the element translates +2px toward the shadow, which simultaneously shrinks the shadow by 2px — creating a physical "press into surface" sensation without any JavaScript. This works at CSS-only level and pairs with framer-motion's existing whileTap for full press feedback.
